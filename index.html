The Best NBA Playoff Performances of All Time

The NBA playoffs is where the best players truly shine. Legends are made in the postseason. Throughout the history of the NBA, some of the best players to ever step on the court have had all-time great individual performances in the playoffs. In this list, you will find 10 of the best individual performances in NBA playoff history, from most recent to oldest.

Luka Doncic's Historic Game Winner

Doncic's performance in Game 4 of the Western First Round in 2020 broke so many records that it's impossible to list them all here, so you can find them here instead. His OT buzzer beater against the Clippers capped off a 21 point comeback for the Mavs, the largest in franchise postseason history. His monster triple-double made him only the ninth player to ever record consecutive triples doubles in the playoffs, and only the third at the age of 21 or younger.

LeBron James brings the Cavs back from down 3-1 in the Finals

James' chasedown block of Andre Igoudala in the closing minutes of Game 7 of the 2016 NBA Finals is one of the most iconic moments in NBA history. The moment capped off the Cavs comeback from down 3-1 against the 73-9 Warriors, which made them the only team to ever come back from a 3-1 deficit in the Finals. James' triple-double in Game 7 also capped off a series of huge performances, and allowed him to finally bring a title to Cleveland.

LeBron's Best Playoff Game Ever?

In 2012, two years into the "Big 3" era in Miami, the Heat still hadn't won a championship. And now, they were facing elimination to the Boston Celtics, one of James' long time rivals, in Game 6 of the Eastern Conference Finals. If the Heat lost here, they would have nothing to show two years after bringing 3 All-Stars together. With the pressure on, James had a monster performance, scoring 45 points and grabbing 15 rebounds, pushing the series to a Game 7 back in Miami. The Heat would go on to win Game 7 and the championship, James’ first. This game is considered the greatest of James’ career by many.

A Young LeBron Carries the Cavs through Double Overtime

In 2007, at age 22, in just his second ever postseason, LeBron James led the Cavs to the Eastern Conference Finals against the Detroit Pistons, who were in their fifth consecutive Conference Finals and were only 2 years removed from winning a championship. In Game 5, with 6:14 left in regulation, the Cavaliers were up 79-78. Through the end of regulation and both overtimes, James scored 29 of the Cav’s final 30 points, including the team’s last 25 points, to lead them to victory. The Cavs would go on to win Game 6 and make it to their first Finals in franchise history, which was also the first Finals appearance of many for James.

Allen Iverson upsets the Kobe and Shaq led Lakers

When reigning MVP AI and the ragtag Sixers went up against the powerhouse Lakers in the 2001 Finals, everybody thought that it would be a fast and easy sweep for the Lakers. That is, everybody not named Allen Iverson. The Lakers had reached the Finals with an 11-0 record, sweeping all three teams they faced in the Western Conference Playoffs. They would have ended with a perfect record in the postseason if not for AI’s monster performance in Game 1 of the Finals, where he played all but three seconds of the overtime victory. Even though the Sixers would go on to lose the series, Game 1 showed that AI was truly the MVP and also produced one of the most iconic moments in NBA history (link).

Michael Jordan’s “Last Shot”

MJ is one the greatest players to ever touch a basketball. He won two different three-peats with the Bulls, totaling to six titles in eight years. 1998 was the culmination of the second three-peat for Jordan and the Bulls, and they had to face a tough Utah Jazz team in the Finals. With Scottie Pippen, Jordan’s all-time great sidekick, barely able to move due to an injury, Jordan made sure that the Bulls finished out their second three-peat with a 45 point performance in Game 6, including the iconic game winning shot (link) over Byron Russel. Whild Jordan would play again for the Washington Wizards in the early 2000s, this shot was the culmination of an era of basketball dominated by MJ.

The Flu Game

A performance so iconic that it needs no other introduction. In 1997, Jordan’s Bulls on John Stockon, Karl Malone, and the Utah Jazz in the Finals. In the hours leading up to Game 5, Jordan did not participate in practice and was in a dark locker room vomiting. However, he told Coach Phil Jackson he could play, and ended up playing 44 minutes and dropping 38, with 15 in the fourth quarter, in a win that sent the series back to Chicago with the Bulls up 3-2. The Flu Game is by far the most iconic and memorable playoff performance in NBA history.

“It’s just God disguised as Michael Jordan”

Before winning six rings in eight years, Michael Jordan was tasked with dragging the abysmal Bulls team that drafted him through an Eastern Conference that was made up of some all-time great teams. One such team was the 1986 Boston Celtics, featuring 3-time MVP Larry Bird. MJ and Bulls ran into the Celtics in the first round of the 1986 playoffs. In Game 2, MJ dropped 63 points in a double-overtime loss, which broke Elgin Baylor’s 24-year-old record for most points in a playoff, and is a record that still stands today. After the game, Larry Bird said that MJ was the greatest player he had ever seen and called him “God disguised as Michael Jordan.”

Magic Johnson Wins Finals MVP as a Rookie

While Magic Johnson played point guard, he was incredibly tall, standing at 6’9”. This came in handy for Game 6 of the 1980 NBA Finals, where Johnson, Kareem Abdul-Jabbar, and the Lakers were in the midst of taking on the Philadelphia 76ers. With Abdul-Jabbar out due to an ankle injury, Johnson began the game at center but played every position throughout the game and led the Lakers to a 123-107 victory, winning them the title, their seventh in franchise history. Johnson’s performance earned him Final MVP, making him the only rookie in NBA history to earn such an honor.

Bill Russell Stuffs the Stat Sheet

Bill Russell is the winningest player in NBA history, having won 11 championships with the Boston Celtics. He is also responsible for some of the most eye-popping performances in NBA playoff history. His most dazzling performance was in Game 7 of the 1962 NBA Finals, where his Celtics took on the Lakers. He played every minute of the game, including overtime, and finished with 30 points and 40 rebounds. Since blocks weren’t recorded in thoses days of the NBA, we can only imagine how many he had if he pulled down 40 rebounds. Russell’s performance clinched Boston’s fourth straight championship, and the fifth in franchise history. Russell and the Celtics would go on to face the Lakers in the Finals five more times throughout the 1960s, and Boston would win each time.

The Best NBA Playoff Performances of All Time

List of performances Player Images Player Stats

The NBA playoffs is where the best players truly shine. Legends are made in the postseason. Throughout the history of the NBA, some of the best players to ever step on the court have had all-time great individual performances in the playoffs. In this list, you will find 10 of the best individual performances in NBA playoff history.

Luka Doncic's Historic Game Winner

  • Points: 43
  • Assists: 13
  • Rebounds: 17
  • Blocks: 1
  • Steals: 2
  • FG%: 18/31

Doncic's performance in Game 4 of the Western First Round in 2020 broke so many records that it's impossible to list them all here, so you can find them here instead. His OT buzzer beater against the Clippers capped off a 21 point comeback for the Mavs, the largest in franchise postseason history. His monster triple-double made him only the ninth player to ever record consecutive triples doubles in the playoffs, and only the third at the age of 21 or younger.

LeBron James brings the Cavs back from down 3-1 in the Finals

  • Points: 27
  • Assists: 11
  • Rebounds: 11
  • Blocks: 3
  • Steals: 2
  • FG%: 9/24

James' chasedown block of Andre Igoudala in the closing minutes of Game 7 of the 2016 NBA Finals is one of the most iconic moments in NBA history. The moment capped off the Cavs comeback from down 3-1 against the 73-9 Warriors, which made them the only team to ever come back from a 3-1 deficit in the Finals. James' triple-double in Game 7 also capped off a series of huge performances, and allowed him to finally bring a title to Cleveland.

LeBron's Best Playoff Game Ever?

  • Points: 45
  • Assists: 5
  • Rebounds: 15
  • Blocks: 0
  • Steals: 0
  • FG%: 19/26

In 2012, the Heat were facing elimination at the hands of the Boston Celtics, one of James' long time rivals, in Game 6 of the Eastern Conference Finals. If the Heat lost here, they would have nothing to show two years after bringing 3 All-Stars together. With the pressure on, James had a monster performance, which pushed the series to a Game 7 back in Miami. The Heat would go on to win Game 7 and the championship, James’ first. Many people consider this game the greatest of James’ career.

A Young LeBron Carries the Cavs through Double Overtime

  • Points: 48
  • Assists: 7
  • Rebounds: 9
  • Blocks: 0
  • Steals: 2
  • FG%: 18/33

In 2007, at age 22, LeBron James led the Cavs to the Eastern Conference Finals against the Detroit Pistons, who were in their fifth consecutive Conference Finals. With the Cavs holding a one point lead with six minutes left in Game 5, James scored 29 of the Cav’s final 30 points, including the team’s last 25 points, to lead them to victory in double overtime. The Cavs would go on to win Game 6 and make it to their and James’ first Finals.

Allen Iverson upsets the Kobe and Shaq led Lakers

  • Points: 48
  • Assists: 6
  • Rebounds: 5
  • Blocks: 0
  • Steals: 5
  • FG%: 18/41

When reigning MVP AI and the ragtag Sixers went up against the powerhouse Lakers in the 2001 Finals, everybody thought that it would be a fast and easy sweep for the Lakers. That is, everybody not named Allen Iverson. The Lakers would have ended the postseason with a perfect record if not for AI’s monster performance in Game 1 of the Finals, where he played all but three seconds of the overtime victory. This upset also produced one of the most iconic moments in NBA history.

Michael Jordan’s “Last Shot”

  • Points: 45
  • Assists: 1
  • Rebounds: 1
  • Blocks: 0
  • Steals: 4
  • FG%: 15/35

1998 was the culmination of the second three-peat for Jordan and the Bulls, and they had to face a tough Utah Jazz team in the Finals. With Scottie Pippen, Jordan’s all-time great sidekick, barely able to move due to an injury, Jordan made sure that the Bulls finished out their second three-peat with a 45 point performance in Game 6, including the iconic game winning shot over Byron Russel. While Jordan would play again for the Washington Wizards in the early 2000s, this shot was the culmination of an era of basketball dominated by MJ.

The Flu Game

  • Points: 38
  • Assists: 5
  • Rebounds: 7
  • Blocks: 1
  • Steals: 3
  • FG%: 13/27

A performance so iconic that it needs no other introduction. In 1997, Jordan’s Bulls took on John Stockon, Karl Malone, and the Utah Jazz in the Finals. In the hours leading up to Game 5, Jordan did not participate in practice and was in a dark locker room vomiting. However, he told Coach Phil Jackson he could play, and ended up playing 44 minutes and dropping 38 points, with 15 in the fourth quarter, in a win that sent the series back to Chicago with the Bulls up 3-2. The Flu Game is by far the most iconic and memorable playoff performance in NBA history.

“It’s just God disguised as Michael Jordan”

  • Points: 63
  • Assists: 6
  • Rebounds: 5
  • Blocks: 2
  • Steals: 3
  • FG%: 22/41

In his early years, Jordan faced some tough teams in the Eastern Conference. One such team was the 1986 Boston Celtics, featuring 3-time MVP Larry Bird. MJ and the Bulls ran into the Celtics in the first round of the 1986 playoffs. In Game 2, MJ dropped 63 points in a double-overtime loss, which broke Elgin Baylor’s 24-year-old record for the most points in a playoff game. After the game, Larry Bird said that MJ was the greatest player he had ever seen and called him “God disguised as Michael Jordan.”

Magic Johnson Wins Finals MVP as a Rookie

  • Points: 42
  • Assists: 7
  • Rebounds: 15
  • Blocks: 1
  • Steals: 3
  • FG%: 14/23

In 1980, rookie Magic Johnson, Kareem Abdul-Jabbar, and the Lakers were in the midst of taking on the Philadelphia 76ers in the Finals. With Abdul-Jabbar out for Game 6 due to an ankle injury, Johnson began the game at center but played every position throughout the game and led the Lakers to a 123-107 victory, winning them their seventh title in franchise history. Johnson’s performance earned him Finals MVP, making him the only rookie in NBA history to recieve the award.

Bill Russell Stuffs the Stat Sheet

  • Points: 30
  • Assists: 4
  • Rebounds: 40
  • Blocks: N/A
  • Steals: N/A
  • FG%: 8/18

Bill Russell, who won an NBA record 11 championships with the Boston Celtics, is responsible for some of the most eye-popping performances in NBA playoff history. His most dazzling performance was in Game 7 of the 1962 NBA Finals, where his Celtics took on the Lakers. He played every minute of the game, including overtime, and finished with 30 points and 40 rebounds. Russell’s performance clinched Boston’s fourth straight championship.
